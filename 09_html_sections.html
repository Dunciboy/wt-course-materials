  • Traditionally, these elements were used to mark parts of a document:
    <div>

  • <div> is a blocklevel element (like paragraphs)
  • <span> is an inline element (like characters)

SECTIONS

Sectioning — HTML5 style

New HTML5 elements

  • This works, but <div> has no semantic meaning
  • HTML5 comes with four new sectioning elements:
    <section>
    <nav>
    <article>
    <aside>
  • and two additional elements to be used within sections:
    <header>
    <footer>
  • There's still a lot of debate and tweaking on when and how to use these elements, but the latest W3C HTML5.1 nightly draft, albeit not perfect, looks solid and usable enough.

<div>

  • <div> is still used, but for layout, styling or scripting only
  • use for:
    • positioning and styling fragments: page or main content wrapper, cells in a grid layout...
    • container for scripts like google map
  • don't use:
    • where another more semantic element would be more appropriate: <section>, <p>, <blockquote>, <figure>, <header>, <nav>...

<section>

  • marks a thematic group of content that starts with a heading
  • use for:
    • chapters of a tutorial
    • tabs of a tabbed content
    • introduction, news items and contact information areas on the homepage
    • comments area of an article
    • basically anything that starts with a heading
  • don't use:
    • as hook for styling or scripting (use <div> instead)
    • if <aside>, <nav> or <article> is more appropriate

Quotes from the W3C:

A general rule is that the section element is appropriate (...) if the element's contents would be listed explicitly in the document's outline.
The section element is not a generic container element. When an element is needed only for styling purposes or as a convenience for scripting, authors are encouraged to use the div element instead.

<article>

  • piece of content reusable in another context or website
  • is a special case of <section>
  • use for:
    • a blogpost, tutorial or newspaper article
    • a tweet
    • a comment
    • an advertisement or banner
    • a widget: poll, calendar, latest comments...
  • don't use for:
    • a part of a blogpost
    • a login form
    • a related links section
  • if not sure, use <section>

Literally from the W3C:

The article element represents a complete, or self-contained, composition in a document, page, application, or site and that is, in principle, independently distributable or reusable, e.g. in syndication. This could be a forum post, a magazine or newspaper article, a blog entry, a user-submitted comment, an interactive widget or gadget, or any other independent item of content.

<nav>

  • marks the main navigation blocks in a page
  • is a special case of <section>
  • contains a group of links only: no single links, forms, filters...
  • use for:
    • main and submenu
    • breadcrumb
    • paging
    • group of contextual links
  • don't use for:
    • language menu
    • filters, facet navigation
    • search form
    • hash tags, tag clouds
    • footer links
    • calls to action

Literally from the W3C:

The nav element represents a section of a page that links to other pages or to parts within the page: a section with navigation links.
Not all groups of links on a page need to be in a nav element — the element is primarily intended for sections that consist of major navigation blocks.

<aside>

  • piece of non-essential content related to the site
  • if nested within <article>: related to the article only
  • is a special case of <section>
  • use for:
    • related advertising, links or products
    • 'recenty viewed' or 'others also recommend' sections
    • short background information or quick facts
    • a blogroll
    • a pull quote
  • don't use for:
    • non-related advertising
    • major navigation blocks
    • where <header>, <footer> or <nav> would be more appropriate

Literally from the W3C:

The aside element represents a section of a page that consists of content that is tangentially related to the content around the aside element, and which could be considered separate from that content. Such sections are often represented as sidebars in printed typography.
The element can be used for typographical effects like pull quotes or sidebars, for advertising, for groups of nav elements, and for other content that is considered separate from the main content of the page.

<header>

  • introductory content at the top of a sectioning element, or <body> if this is the nearest ancestor
  • may contain:
    • a heading
    • navigation elements
    • a short introduction or summary
    • a logo
    • information about the writer, post date...

Literally from the W3C:

The header element represents introductory content for its nearest ancestor sectioning content or sectioning root element. A header typically contains a group of introductory or navigational aids.
When the nearest ancestor sectioning content or sectioning root element is the body element, then it applies to the whole page.

<footer>

  • closing content usually at the bottom of a sectioning element, or <body> if this is the nearest ancestor
  • not necessarily at the bottom, e.g. comments may follow
  • may contain:
    • paging, further reading and other navigation elements
    • short conclusion
    • information about the writer, copyright

Literally from the W3C:

The footer element represents a footer for its nearest ancestor sectioning content or sectioning root element. A footer typically contains information about its section such as who wrote it, links to related documents, copyright data, and the like.

Sectioning — Howto

Headings and sections

  • <section>, <article>, <nav>, <aside> are sections
  • <body> is considered the root section
  • sections and headings are closely related:
    • a section starts with a heading
    • a heading starts a new section
  • at the end we should get a nice outline (plugin or online tool):

Not all elements contribute to the outline: <blockquote>, <details>, <dialog>, <fieldset>, <figure> and <td> are considered sectioning roots that have their own outlines.

Headings and sections

  • Here's how to create proper sections and headings:
    • first create an outline for your document (on paper!)
      • the root is the site title
      • if a heading is missing on the screenshot, choose a good one yourself
    • decide which sections are articles, navs, asides or just sections
    • the outline decides the sections and headings:
      • the structure and nestings in the outline is the same as the sections in HTML
      • the outline indent level is the same as the heading number in HTML
    • finally hide the headings you don't need in the final design (see next slide)
  • Now you may add <header> and / or <footer> to each section if you wish.

Implicit sections (1)

  • Here's a little secret: you don't have to specify a <section> if you don't want to; a new heading autmatically starts a new section. This is called an implicit section. With this we can simplify a few things.
  • If a <section> is the only child of its parent for example: 
    ...
<footer>
  <section>
    <h2 class="structural">Footer</h2>
    ...
  </section>
</footer>
...

    ...
<footer>
  <h2 class="structural">Footer</h2>
  ...
</footer>
...

Implicit sections (2)

  • Or if sections are just small parts of a single content: 
    ...
<section>
  <h4>Veggies es bonus</h4>
  ...
</section>
<section>
  <h4>Turnip greens</h4>
  ...
</section>
<section>
  <h4>Nori grape</h4>
  ...
</section>
...
    simplify it to: 
    ...
<h4>Veggies es bonus</h4>
...
<h4>Turnip greens</h4>
...
<h4>Nori grape</h4>
...

It must be said that the W3C does not encourage implicit sections. A quote:

Authors are also encouraged to explicitly wrap sections in elements of sectioning content, instead of relying on the implicit sections generated by having multiple headings in one element of sectioning content.

Heading renumbering (1)

  • Here's another little secret: actually a sequential numbering of your headings is not required. You may name all headers <h1> if you wish, or even in reverse order; it will produce the same outline.
  • Do not mix implicit sections with heading renumbering too much, or your outliner will get confused. You may have to try and error a little to get the desired outline.

Some more quotes from the W3C:

Notice how the use of section means that the author can use h1 elements throughout, without having to worry about whether a particular section is at the top level, the second level, the third level, and so on.
Sections may contain headings of any rank, and authors are strongly encouraged to use headings of the appropriate rank for the section's nesting level.

Heading renumbering (2)

  • We will use this freedom to restart the heading numbering from <h1> within an <article>, as it is meant to be distributable, but only if it is from an external source, e.g. advertisement, tweet etc...
  • In our example: 
    ...
<aside>
  <h4 class="structural">Side notes</h4>
  ...
  <article>
    <h1>Advertisement</h1>
    ...
  </article>
</aside>
...

Heading renumbering (3)

  • But wait! How about search engines? Shouldn't the highest heading numbers be assigned to the most important headings?
  • Errr... true, when producing a site today, it may be better to forget about outlines and correct heading numbering, and assign numbers according to importance
  • My guess: in one or two years search engine indexers will be HTML5 and outline aware, and might even bonus your site for a correct outline. So start learning the correct numbering now, and use the old style numbering if you throw a site online today

It must be said that search engines are rather vague about the effect of HTML5 header numbering on your ranking. An interesting read on http://html5doctor.com/html5-seo-search-engine-optimisation/ and a Google comment on http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GIn5qJKU8VM

Summary

  • Your document should generate a correct outline; this is done with a correct section nesting and header numbering.
  • You may use implicit sections and / or heading renumbering, but don't mix the two too much or your outline will break.
  • You basically have three options:
    • You forget about correct outlining, and use sections, headings and heading numbers as feels right for you and search engines (not recommended)
    • You go for a correct ouline, but keep it search engine friendly: assign heading numbers according to importance, and don't use implicit sections or your outline will break (recommended in production).
    • You go for a correct ouline, and follow the W3C recommendation: assign heading numbers according to nesting level. You may use implicit sections if you wish, but don't overdo. Header renumbering, e.g. in <article> is permitted and encouraged, but be careful not to mix it with implicit sections or your outline might break (recommended in exercises).

Grouping content

Grouping content (1)

things that belong together, should be grouped together

Grouping content (2)

  • Examples of things that belong together:
    • controls, title, help texts etc.. of a single form
    • group of social media icons
    • avatar, title, date, text etc... of a single comment
    • picture and caption
    • contact information
    • introductory content: logo, top menu, login...
  • They should be wrapped in a common parent:
    • <form>
    • <ul>
    • <article>
    • <figure>
    • <dl>, <p> or <address>
    • <header>

Example: bottom menu

  • wrong: 
    <a href="#">about</a> | <a href="#">disclaimer</a> | <a href="#">contact</a>
  • better: 
    #bottommenu li + li:before {
  content: "|";
  margin: 0.5em;
}

    <ul id="bottommenu">
  <li><a href="#">about</a></li>
  <li><a href="#">disclaimer</a></li>
  <li><a href="#">contact</a></li>
<ul>
